About Me


I am a University Research Scholar pursuing my PhD in the Intelligent Systems Cluster at the ANU School of Computing in the College of Engineering, Computing and Cybernetics, The Australian National University since March, 2023. I am currently working on 3D diffusion modelling for sparse input VSLAM problems in dynamic and uncertain environments.

My supervisors are Dr. Jing Zhang, Prof. Lexing Xie of the Computational Media Lab at ANU with my co-advisor Distinguished Professor Emeritus Richard Hartley. My associate supervisors are Prof. Robert Mahony, STR Lab, ANU and Prof. Matt Garratt at the School of Engineering & IT at UNSW, Canberra.

Education

I am a recent graduate of the Australian National University in the College of Engineering, Computing and Cybernetics. I majored in Mechatronics Systems, with minors in Electronic Communication Systems and Mathematics with a First Class Honours, third in my cohort (Major branch). Experiences

Undergraduate Research Projects

Over my undergraduation I have taken multiple research projects as a part of my training and coursework and some independently. The following six standout among them:

1. Closed Loop Inertial Gradient Dynamics, School of Engineering, ANU, 2021
Undertaken as my Undergraduate Thesis project under the supervision of Prof. Ian Petersen. In this honours project, we investigated the Nesterov ODE developed using symplectic integration by Prof. Su, Boyd and Candes and devised feedback optimization methods that led to the discovery of the Whiplash inertial gradient dynamical system. This state-of-the-art algorithm is deterministic and is exponentially faster than Nesterov-like classical methods for convex functions. We also used control principles to establish universal Lyapunov-based methods to predict rates of convergences of accelerated inertial methods for high-resolution ODE-based approaches.

2. Neural Network for inverting high dimensional functions, School of Computing, ANU, 2022
Undertaken as under the supervision of Distinguished Prof. Richard Hartley. In this project, we took a Neural Network perspective to realize the feasibility of inverting differentiable functions within a closed sample range for non-linear processes. We found that such a phenomenon is restricted to an RMSE hit rate of 72% and requires computationally intensive positional embedding. We discovered that neural networks could not compute inverses of functions unless the embedded function is locally invertible for a smooth manifold. We thus proved inconsistency in the network over its global invertibility.

3. Passive Radar Signal Processing, ARDE, DRDO, 2020
Undertaken as under the supervision of Dr Pranab Saha and Dr V Venkateshwara Rao at the Centre of Advanced Systems in the Armament Research and Development Establishment in the Defence Research and Development Organization. Noisy Signals often have distortion loss, reducing the usefulness of the information it carries. In this project, we developed a Kalman filter-based method to select with a few iterations, using the ambiguity function of a returning radar signal, the matched filter of the radar signal. This method allowed us to optimize the next burst to reduce the uncertainty measure. We use an FPGA-based multi-processor interfacing setup for long-range noisy radar signal processing. Certain aspects of this research are classified.

4. Real-Time Complexity of First-Order Optimization, CIICADA Labs, ANU, 2022
In this project, undertaken under the supervision of Prof. Iman Shames, we study the total complexity of smooth unconstrained deterministic optimization algorithms using first-order algorithms. The lower bound complexity of a problem dictates the number of oracle calls. However, the computational effort of calling the oracle dictates the algorithm's computational complexity. We are essentially trying to develop this idea of the total complexity of the algorithm to determine the net computational effort to reach the minimum using an iteration bound.

5. Saddle Point Optimization with structural uncertainty, Mathematical Sciences Institute, ANU, Ongoing
In this project, undertaken under supervision of Prof. Qinian Jin, we study the stochastic inertial gradient differential equations that model the widely used ADAM in continuous time. We utilise the gradient information and an uncertain Hessian vector product to compute perturbation functions online, which allow escaping saddle points in N-player Minimax games.

6. Bifurcation analysis using Optical Signal Processing through Viscoelastic Graphene Sheet, School of Advanced Sciences, VIT, 2019
In this project, undertaken under supervision of Prof. Gargi Chackraborty, we tried to find a model using parametric state estimations for the projection arguments for a viscoelastic graphene sheet's response under stress. We aimed to explain the irreproducibility of the Bubnov--Galerkin treatment in the presence of optical excitations through the medium. We successfully explained the bifurcations using a non-linear Lyapunov excitation term that took the signal's energy as a singular argument and experimentally verified it for larger gains using the noisy Brueller model.
